DLR 5: Dispelling Resistor Combination Errors

Combining resistors that are in series or parallel is a strategy that is often helpful in analyzing circuits with a single source and multiple resistors. To do so successfully requires that one properly identifies when resistors are in series or in parallel. Let's start with a series connection. While it is sometimes the case that resistors that are in series are tied together at one node, this is neither a necessary nor sufficient condition. Consider the following two circuit in which we will try to identify resistors that are in series.

In the circuit on the left, there is only one current. This current is the same in the voltage source and the three resistors; no matter where the current is measured, one would get the same value. For elements to be in series, they must share the same current. This is the necessary and sufficient condition. Therefore, all four elements in the circuit on the left are in series. When simplifying circuits using series combinations, we deal with resistors. Therefore we focus on the fact that resistors A, B, and C are in series. Notice that while resistors A and B are tied together at one node resistors A and C are not even though resistors A and C are in series. Again, we are looking for resistors that share the same current. Let's keep this in mind when considering the circuit on the right.

While it is true that resistors D and E are tied together at one node, these resistors are not in series as KCL at the node connecting resistors D and E reveals that ID = IE + IF and not that ID = IE. By similar reasoning, while resistors D and F share a single node, they are not in series. Note also that resistors E and F do not have the same current and so are not in series. Interestingly, the resistors that have no physical connection in the circuit, RE and RG, do share the same current (IG=I=ID) and so are in series.

Notice that when combining resistors in series that the equivalent resistance is the sum of the series resistors. Series connections of resistors are therefore useful when we want to increase resistance. While we were able to reduce one of the circuits to a single resistor, we need to explore parallel combinations of resistors to reduce the second circuit further to achieve a single equivalent resistance. Notice that resistors E and F are tied together at two nodes. Resistors tied together at two nodes are in parallel and may be combined to achieve a single, equivalent resistance. To make the situation a bit more interesting when considering parallel combinations, let's explore two new circuits.

In the circuit on the left, we have two resistors in parallel and in the circuit on the right, three resistors in parallel. Again, resistors in parallel are tied together at both ends -- they share two nodes. Where the beginning student may get into trouble is interpreting the formula for the equivalent resistance of resistors in parallel. Typically, the equivalent resistance formula is written in terms of conductances -- that is the inverse of resistance. Here are the formulas for the two circuits we are considering.

Req,left=(1/R1+1/R2)-1     Req,left=(1/R1+1/R2+1/R3)-1

Notice that the circuit on the right is simply that on the left with resistor R3 added in parallel to the other parallel branches. By adding a third resistor, have we increased the resistance? Let's think about what the two formulas suggest. First, we compare the terms in the parentheses.

Req,left=(1/R1+1/R2)  Vs.  (1/R1+1/R2+1/R3)

Which term in the parentheses is larger? Clearly, the term on the right is larger as we have added a 1/R3 term. Does this mean by adding a third parallel branch that we have increased the resistance? Quite the opposite in fact. By adding a third branch we have increased the net conductance. To find the net resistance, we take the inverse of the net conductance and therefore,

Req,left > Req,right

Beyond analyzing the formula, how can we understand this? Resistor R3 was added in parallel thus providing charge another path through which to flow. This makes it easier for charge to flow, thus lowering the overall resistance. Let's put numbers to the two cases to verify this finding as well as to demonstrate a key idea to remember regarding parallel resistors.

Let's assume R1 = 1000 Ω, R2 = 2000 Ω, and R3 = 3000 Ω.

Req,left = (1/1000 + 1/2000)-1 ~ 667 Ω

Req,right = (1/1000 + 1/2000 + 1/3000) ~ 545 Ω

As we expected, adding the third parallel branch reduced the overall resistance. Another item worth remembering is that the effective resistance of a parallel combination of resistors is always smaller than the smallest of the resistors comprising the parallel combination. In both cases above, since the smallest resistor value in the parallel combination is 1000 Ω, we know that the equivalent resistance of the parallel combination will be less than 1000 Ω.
