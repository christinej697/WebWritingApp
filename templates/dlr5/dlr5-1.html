DLR 5: Dispelling Resistor Combination Errors

Combining resistors that are in series or parallel is a strategy that is often helpful in analyzing circuits with a single source and multiple resistors. To do so successfully requires that one properly identifies when resistors are in series or in parallel. Let's start with a series connection. While it is sometimes the case that resistors that are in series are tied together at one node, this is neither a necessary nor sufficient condition. Consider the following two circuits in which we will try to identify resistors that are in series.

In the circuit on the left, there is only one current. This current is the same in the voltage source and the three resistors; no matter where the current is measured, one would get the same value. For elements to be in series, they must share the same current. This is the necessary and sufficient condition. Therefore, all four elements in the circuit on the left are in series. When simplifying circuits using series combinations, we deal with resistors. Therefore, we focus on the fact that resistors A, B, and C are in series. Notice that while resistors A and B are tied together at one node, resistors A and C are not, even though resistors A and C are in series.
