DLR 2: Dispelling the Ideal Independent Voltage Source Misconception

In the circuits below, the same 10V ideal independent voltage source is connected to a 100 Ohm resistor (left circuit) and a 1000 Ohm resistor (right circuit). In both cases, the voltage source maintains a 10V potential difference across its terminals and thus across the sole resistor in each circuit. What is different about the two cases? Ohm's law reveals that the circuit in the left has a current of 100 mA (10 V/100 Ω), whereas that on the right only 10 mA (10 V/ 1000 Ω).

In both cases, the potential difference across the voltage source is 10V with the plus side 10 V higher than the minus sign. The current associated with the source is different in the two cases. Thus, the correct way to model the ideal independent voltage source is just that, a source of a consistent potential difference across its terminals. The current associated with the voltage source will depend on the circuit to which the source is connected.

What about power and the ideal independent voltage source? We know that for any element, P=VI. Therefore, for the circuit on the left, the ideal voltage source provides P= (10V)(0.1A)= 1 W of power. Since the circuit on the right has a current 1/10th that of the circuit on the left, the voltage source needs only to provide 0.1 W of power in that case.

To summarize, an ideal independent voltage source provides a specific potential difference across its terminals regardless of the circuit to which it is connected. The current, and thus the power associated with the ideal independent voltage source will depend on the circuit to which the source is connected.
