DLR 7: Dispelling Energy Conservation Misconceptions

Energy is conserved in an electric circuit. Since power is simply energy per unit time, power is also conserved in an electric circuit. You mentioned the notion of energy (power) conservation in your response and so let's review how it applies to the circuit we are considering.

Applied to an electric circuit, the principle of conservation of energy states that energy is neither created nor destroyed in an electric circuit and is only transformed. For example, in our circuit the voltage source provides energy to the circuit and the resistors dissipate the energy thus heating up in the process. Perhaps the voltage source models a battery with stored chemical energy that is transformed to electrical energy. In analyzing electric circuits, we are typically not concerned with details such as chemical-to-electrical energy conversion and rather only seek to verify that the sum of energy (i.e. power) dissipated or stored is precisely balanced by the amount of energy provided.

It is important to acknowledge that we apply the principle of conservation of energy to the circuit as a whole and not to a particular element. Therefore, it is incorrect to attempt to apply the notion of conservation of energy to a single element whether it be a voltage source, resistor, or any other element in an electric circuit. Some may be confused by the term “ideal independent voltage source” thinking that such a source supplies a consistent amount of power (i.e. energy per unit time) regardless of the circuit to which it is connected. On the contrary, an ideal independent voltage source provides a consistent potential difference across its terminals; the amount of power associated with it depends on the circuit to which it is connected.

The proper way to apply conservation of energy to the circuit in question would be to verify the required balance between the amount of power provided by source and the total amount of power dissipated by the three resistors. We calculate power more frequently than energy as recall that the power associated with a given element is the product of the potential difference across the element and the current in the element. As the resistance of R2 changes, the voltage source may need to provide more or less power. What we know is that the balance between the power supplied and the power dissipated will be maintained within the circuit as a whole. Once again, thinking of a circuit as a system is helpful.
