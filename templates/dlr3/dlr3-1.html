DLR 3: Dispelling the Sequential Misconception

It is not uncommon for the student to exhibit a so-called “sequential“ misconception in explaining the behavior of a circuit such as we are considering. Based on your response, we want to make sure you think of a circuit as more of a system than as a simple connection of independent elements. Here is the circuit again in which we are to address what happens to the power in all four elements when the resistance of R2 decreases.

Someone displaying sequential reasoning might say that since resistor R2 comes after R1 in the circuit, or since R2 is further downstream than R1 the voltage and current associated with R1 are not affected by changes in R2. Such an explanation is contrary to what actually occurs in the circuit with changes in the value of R2. Let's try to understand the situation more correctly by beginning with an exploration of what the ideal independent voltage source does in the circuit, as misconceptions in how to model the voltage source may also come into play with the sequential misconception.

We are told that the voltage source is an ideal independent voltage source, but what does this mean? The key to remember is that an ideal independent voltage source maintains a constant potential difference across its terminals regardless of what elements are connected to it. That is, in the circuit in question we can be confident that the top terminal of the voltage source is precisely Vs volts above its lower terminal. Even as R2 changes, the voltage source will maintain this potential difference across its own terminals. While the voltage difference Vs doesn't change, the current and the power associated with the ideal independent voltage source may change. Let's think about what this potential difference supplied by a voltage source does in a resistive circuit.
