DLR 3: Dispelling the Sequential Misconception

It is not uncommon for the student to exhibit a so-called “sequential “ misconception in explaining the behavior of a circuit such as we are considering. Based on your response, we want to make sure you think of a circuit more of a system than as a simple connection of independent elements. Here is the circuit again in which we are to address what happens to the power in all four elements when the resistance of R2 decreases.

Someone displaying sequential reasoning might say that “since resistor R2 comes after R1 in the circuit”, or since “R2 is further downstream than R1” the voltage and current associated with R1 are not affected by changes in R2. Such an explanation is contrary to what actually occurs in the circuit with changes in the value of R2. Let's try to understand the situation more correctly by beginning with an exploration of what the ideal independent voltage source does in the circuit as misconceptions in how to model the voltage source may also come into play with the sequential misconception.

We are told that the voltage source is an ideal independent voltage source, but what does this mean? The key to remember is that an ideal independent voltage source maintains a constant potential difference across its terminals regardless of what elements are connected to it. That is, in the circuit in question we can be confident that the top terminal of the voltage source is precisely Vs volts above its lower terminal. Even as R2 changes, the voltage source will maintain this potential difference across its own terminals. While the voltage difference Vs doesn't change, the current and the power associated with the ideal independent voltage source may change. Let's think about what this potential difference supplied by a voltage source does in a resistive circuit.

So not to miss the point we want to make, let's consider first a somewhat simpler circuit as shown below. The circuit contains an ideal independent source and two resistors forming a single loop circuit.

Ohm's Law states that current associated with a resistor is the potential difference across a given resistor divided by the resistance. In this case, a total potential difference of Vs drops across a net resistance of Rx+Ry and therefore, Is = Vs/(Rx+Ry). Since current is the same on both sides of a resistor (neither charge nor current is used up in a resistor),indeed, Is=Ix=Iy in the above circuit. To continue to develop a deeper understanding of the situation and how things might change as a value of a resistor changes, we need to think about surface charge. It is surface charge on the conductors and wires which drives the flow of charge in the wires and resistors.

The potential difference across the voltage source causes surface charges in the wires to shift slightly. Thearrangement of surface charge happens instantaneously and is what creates the electric fields in the wires andresistors that drives charge flow in a given direction resulting in electric current. Since both Rx and Ry carry the same current in the circuit in question and if they have different resistance values, surface charge will arrange such that thepotential difference across the higher-valued resistor will be larger. It takes more “push” to drive current through alarger-valued resistor. To make matters more concrete, let's assume that Vs = 10V, Rx = 500 W and Ry = 500 W. Since the resistors are of equal value, they will have the same potential difference. The circuit below represents the case.

While it is true that the overall resistance in the circuit has decreased, this does not mean that resistors need less potential difference. We must remember that the voltage source is a source of potential difference and not of current. Current in the circuit does not remain the same when Ry changes, the value the potential difference across the voltage source does.

Since the effective resistance seen by the source decreases, the current in the circuit increases. This is a single loop circuit and so all three elements carry the same current. It takes a greater potential difference to enable a given value of current to exist in a larger value resistor than a small value resistor. Therefore, more of the potential difference from the voltage source will drop across Rx than across Ry. Ok, so let's summarize a few things we have learned

(1) We want to think of a circuit as a system - changes in a given part of a circuit may have an impact on every element in a circuit.

(2) An ideal independent voltage source exhibits a constant potential difference across its terminals regardless of what is happening in a circuit.

(3) The current associated with an ideal independent voltage source will change if the effective resistance the source "sees" changes.

(4) The larger the resistance, the greater amount of potential difference it takes to drice a given current in the resistor.
