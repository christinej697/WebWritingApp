DLR 6: Dispelling Precedence of Current Error

Reviewing your response to the question, we want to make certain that you have the accepted understanding of the relationship between voltage and current in an electric circuit. We know that for a resistor, voltage and current are related by Ohm's law as follows:

V = IR

where 'V' is the potential difference across the resistor, 'I' the current in the resistor and 'R' the resistor's value of resistance. The way Ohm's Law has been expressed may lead some to believe that it is the current that causes the potential difference; it is actually the opposite.

The voltage source in the circuit provides the energy needed to shift surface charges in a circuit. This displaced surface charge in turn creates electric fields within a circuit, providing the force needed to drive charge flow. Without such an electric field, the motion of charge in the wires and circuit elements is random. We account for these electric fields using the concept of potential difference. When a potential difference exists across a resistor, for example, the motion of charge in the resistor is no longer random but moves in a concerted direction overall. This is current. From the point of developing the proper intuition, perhaps one should express Ohm's Law as:

I = V/R

Thus suggesting that current is the result of a potential difference and not the other way. Remember that the voltage difference (aka potential difference) between two points in the circuit is just the difference in potential energy per unit charge between those two points.
