DLR 1: Dispelling the Localized Misconception

When beginning to study circuit analysis, it is not uncommon to have a very “localized” conception on how an electric circuit works. In such outlook, one is inclined to think that if a change is made somewhere in the circuit, only the element that is changed is impacted. Let's spend a couple of minutes making certain we look at a circuit as a system and not simply a connection of independent elements. Here is the circuit again in which we are to address what happens to the power in all four elements when the resistance of R2 decreases.

Someone displaying localized reasoning in analyzing the above circuit might say that “as the value of R2 decreases only the power of R2 will be affected.” We can quickly argue against such reasoning by considering what is meant by power conservation in an electric circuit.

Your result:

Incorrect, all four statements regarding power in the circuit in question are true.

Power, which is energy per unit time, is conserved in an electric circuit. This means that there is a precise balance between the power that is provided and that which is dissipated (and/or stored).

If for example, the power associated with R2 decreased as the value of R2 decreased while the power associated with the other elements remained constant, the voltage source would be providing more energy per unit time to the circuit than what is being dissipated -- suggesting that there is not a precise balance in power. If on the other hand, the power associated with R2 increased as the value of R2 decreased while the power associated with the other elements remained constant, there would be more energy per unit time dissipated in the circuit than what is provided! Both of these scenarios are incorrect. Therefore, if one argues that as the resistance of R2 decreases only the power of resistor R2 is affected, that person is suggesting that power is not conserved.

It is important that we come to appreciate that an electric circuit should be considered a system, while a change may be made to one very specific part of an electric, the effects of this change may be felt throughout the circuit!
